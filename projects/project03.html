Market Research

Cartographer is a project that I started after becoming interested in the marketing aspects of a business, particularly in regards to Key Opinion Leaders, Community Leaders, and Influencers. A big portion of marketing nowdays is related to identifying and marketing through these individuals as opposed to things like ads. However, when I inquired about a quantitative method of identifying people like this at my work, it became apparent that much of this is still manually done. Rather then any quantitative approach, it more boiled down into a networking excecise then an analytical one.

Seeing this as a potential avenue for improvement, or even just an alternative way to do things, this project was borne. The goal of this project was to explore quantitative methods for finding Key Opinion Leaders ,map their influence across a topic space, and granularly explore a certain KOL's sphere of influence. These methods could then be hopefully translated into a tool that marketing and market research analysts could then use.

Starting Out

the planning part The first challenge was to figure out what exactly to measure. Influence is a fickle and weird thing to measure. In social media contexts, raw numbers are often used as a substitute, the more followers you have, the more influence you have. However, that ran anti-thetical to what I wanted to build. I wanted granualar analysis, not just reducing people down to a metric. Besides, my initial research into this already revealed that something like this existed, the h-index. The h-index can be understood as a measure of how many citations an author received relative to how many articles they published. Many companies like thompson reuters also had their own propetary measures. (maybe expande more here) And it was here that the first idea struck me. I could implement all the citation relations into a relational database, and then once completed graph algorithms looking at centrality, pathing, etc could then be run to give insights on the community and the individual in it.

Creating the Graph

using neo4j to create an relation database Medical Device development would not be complete without documentation. My work here could be divided into two main categories, R&D Design Control related work and product testing related work.

My Design Control work included authoring the technical drawings for components I was responsible for, and also associated specs in our Product Specification document. Risk documentation was also a significant portion of my work here. I was responsible for authoring Hazard Analyses, and various Failure Mode Effects Analyses cover the design and use of our various devices. Finally, I also provided support for design reviews, authoring everything from memos to powerpoint slides to gap analyses.

Testing work encompassed all the documentation that came with both in-house testing we did, such as functional deployment, and testing performed with outside partners, such as for biocompatibility, corrosion resistance, MRI safety, or Finite Element Analyses. I was responsible for authoring Test Protocols and Reports, as well as Standard Test Methods(STM). For many STMs, I also was responsible for executing a Gauge R&R Study on our test methods. I was in charge of setting up the design of the study, executing it, and then analyzing the data in Minitab, looking at the variance components and the ANOVA report. In certain cases, some test methods had unacceptabily high variance, and so I was also responsible for coming up with solutions to reduce it. Most methods could be improved by simply using more precise equipment, ie swapping a ruler for caliper. However, for certain methods, especially those unique to our device, this entailed having to rethink how the test was executed, or having to implement changes to the test fixture.

Analytics

using the db, ie running Pagerank, etc. Probably the most interesting part of my work was related to supporting Pre-Clinical and Clinical Trials, and not just because I got to travel. Any medical device development is always accompanied by testing both in-vivo and in-vitro. At Prodeon Medical, I had the opportunity to be directly involved in cadaver, animal, and First-in-Human clinical trials.

Organ cadavers were my first exposure to testing outside the bench, and was an invaluable tool in evaluating early designs. Even the best bench models had limitations, and so these tests allowed us to rapidly screen out designs and test the feasibility of our proposed procedure. Our extensive cadaver studies helped immensely in allowing us to succesfully start a longer term animal study within a year or so of the project start.

I was extensively involved our animal trials, using the knowledge gained to evaluate potential designs, and to test the long-term efficacy of the implant. Here I manufactured and prepared devices to be used, worked with the vetinary surgeon to implant the devices, and analyzed the collected data such as x-rays. I was also responsible for defining and executing our post-retrieval testing for the implants, which included mechanical testing, and SEM imaging of the surface.

When the time came for clinical trials, I was on the ground in Europe, Asia, and Australia training the first physicians for the First in Human EXPANDER-1 Trial. I also provided remote technical support and helped execute device deficiency investigations.
